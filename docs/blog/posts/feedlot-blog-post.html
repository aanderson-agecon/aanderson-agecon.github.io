What Happens to Feedlot Performance When Feed Prices Shift?

Feedlots
Feed Costs
Research
Feed price changes don’t just raise costs — they trigger substitution decisions that can meaningfully improve or hurt cattle performance.
Published

September 15, 2025

Feed costs typically account for 60–70% of the total cost of gaining a pound of beef in a feedlot, which makes feedlot managers acutely sensitive to prices for corn, distillers grains, hay, and the many other ingredients that flow in and out of a finishing ration. While the relationship between feed costs and profitability is well-established in the literature, the mechanism through which feed prices affect cattle performance is less understood. In a study I recently published with co-authors James Mitchell and Jaime Luke in the Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics, we examine that mechanism directly.

The key insight motivating the study is that when feed prices shift, profit-maximizing feedlot managers don’t simply absorb the cost change — they adjust. They substitute one ingredient for another in response to changing relative prices, and those substitution decisions alter the composition of the ration, which in turn affects how efficiently cattle convert feed to gain. Accordingly, higher feed prices don’t translate mechanically into worse performance; the outcome depends on what ingredients are substituted and how those substitutes perform on the margin.

Data and Measures of Performance

We used nearly 30 years of monthly closeout data from the Kansas Focus on Feedlots survey — a well-established benchmark for feedlot performance in the Southern Plains — spanning April 1995 through September 2024. We focused on three measures of technical efficiency that feedlot managers track closely: the feed conversion ratio (FCR), average daily gain (ADG), and days on feed (DOF).

The feed conversion ratio measures pounds of feed required per pound of gain, where lower values indicate greater efficiency. Average daily gain measures pounds gained per day, with higher values indicating better performance. Days on feed measures the length of the feeding period, where shorter periods are generally preferred, all else equal. These three measures capture complementary dimensions of feedlot performance and allow us to evaluate how feed price changes affect efficiency through multiple channels.

Separating the Effects of Price Changes

A central contribution of this study is the separation of feed price effects into distinct components. The total effect of a price change on efficiency includes all the ways a feedlot might respond: adjusting ration composition, changing placement weights, and changing slaughter weights. To isolate what is specifically attributable to feed ingredient substitution — independent of changes in cattle weights — we estimated additional model specifications that hold placement weight and slaughter weight constant. The consistent finding across specifications is that feed substitution is the dominant pathway through which price changes affect technical efficiency. It is primarily about what cattle are switched to, not simply whether they are fed more or less.

Key Findings by Ingredient

Results varied considerably across feed ingredients, and several findings were contrary to what one might expect.

Alfalfa price increases were associated with improved FCR and faster average daily gain. Alfalfa is not only a roughage but also a meaningful source of protein and energy, and when its price rises and feedlots substitute away from it, the replacement feeds tend to maintain or improve the diet’s energy and protein density. The result is improved performance on the margin.

Dried distillers grains (DDGs) produced one of the most notable findings from a management standpoint. Increases in DDG prices were associated with significantly more days on feed and slower average daily gain across all model specifications. DDGs are a high-energy, high-protein ethanol by-product that feedlots rely on heavily. When they become relatively more expensive and feedlots substitute to lower-energy alternatives, cattle performance declines measurably. This result underscores the importance of DDGs as a key ingredient for achieving efficient weight gain.

Sorghum and barley price increases were consistently associated with worse FCR and slower gains, suggesting that substitutes for these grains at the margin are less efficient. Conversely, higher soymeal and wheat prices were associated with improved FCR, suggesting that when feedlots substitute away from these ingredients, the replacements are performing more efficiently on the margin.

Corn price changes showed no statistically significant association with FCR, ADG, or days on feed. This is a notable finding given corn’s central role in feedlot rations. Two explanations are plausible. First, demand for corn as a feedlot ingredient may be relatively price-inelastic — feedlots may not reduce corn inclusion substantially even when prices rise, because no close substitute performs as well. Second, when feedlots do substitute away from corn, they tend to use grains with similar nutritional profiles, such as sorghum, and the performance effects largely offset.

Implications for Management and Policy

For feedlot managers, these results offer a clearer picture of what to expect when specific market prices move. Not all feed price increases carry equivalent performance consequences. A spike in DDG prices, for instance, carries real efficiency costs if managers substitute to lower-energy alternatives, whereas increases in alfalfa prices may push the ration toward more efficient substitutes. The efficiency effects estimated here are the result of profit-maximizing decisions already being made by feedlot managers; consequently, this research is not prescriptive. Rather, it provides information that may be useful for planning and logistics as market conditions change.

There are also policy implications to consider. Commodity prices are affected by programs such as the Renewable Fuel Standard, which influences both corn and DDG prices through its effects on ethanol demand. If such policies alter the relative price of DDGs and lead feedlots to substitute toward lower-energy ingredients, the effects on days on feed could translate into changes in total greenhouse gas emissions from fed cattle, given that emissions over a steer’s lifetime are influenced by the length of the feeding period. Our results provide estimates that could be used to evaluate these kinds of unintended consequences in future policy analyses.

Summary

Feed prices affect feedlot technical efficiency primarily through the substitution decisions they trigger, rather than through cost effects alone. The direction and magnitude of those effects vary by ingredient, and in some cases run counter to intuition. DDGs stand out as particularly important for maintaining performance, while corn price changes appear to have little detectable effect on efficiency outcomes. These findings have implications for how feedlot managers interpret market signals and for the evaluation of commodity-related policies that may indirectly affect the cattle feeding sector.
